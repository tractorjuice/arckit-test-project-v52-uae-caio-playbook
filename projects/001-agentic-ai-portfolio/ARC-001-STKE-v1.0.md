## Executive Summary

### Purpose

This document identifies the stakeholders shaping the entity's agentic-AI portfolio (the five directive-named federal pilots), their underlying drivers, the goals those drivers translate into, and the measurable outcomes that will satisfy them. The analysis frames the work in the language of HH Sheikh Mansour's Ministerial Council for AI and Development and the three KPIs of the 23 April 2026 directive: **adoption speed, implementation quality, workflow redesign**.

### Key Findings

The portfolio sits at the intersection of two strong forces that are partially in tension: a federal directive demanding 50% autonomous service delivery by 2028 (driving for adoption speed) and the UAE Charter / PDPL / IAS / Cloud Residency framework demanding rigorous assurance (driving for quality and contestability). The most important conflict to resolve is **Cabinet Affairs' pressure to demonstrate adoption speed against the AI Governance Committee's obligation to certify safety, fairness, and PDPL/IAS compliance before each agent advances tier**. The most important synergy is between the workforce (80,000 in retraining) and the CAIO — both depend on agents being designed to redesign the work, not merely automate it (KPI 3).

### Critical Success Factors

- Five ATRS records published, one per named agent, before the next quarterly Ministerial Council reporting cycle
- Autonomy-tier classification recorded for every agent and enforced through promotion gates
- Quantified contribution to the three directive KPIs visible in the entity's quarterly dashboard
- A working contestation route (Arabic and English, plain language) for every citizen-affecting decision class
- No PDPL or IAS-reportable incident attributable to the portfolio in the first reporting period

### Stakeholder Alignment Score

**Overall Alignment**: MEDIUM–HIGH

Federal stakeholders (Ministerial Council, Cabinet Affairs, Entity Highest Authority) and entity executive stakeholders (CAIO, CISO, DPO) are aligned on the **target** (the directive). They are not yet aligned on the **pace** — Cabinet Affairs is metric-pressured, the assurance functions are evidence-pressured. The proposed resolution is the autonomy-tier gate (PRIN §I.2), which allows fast progress at Tier 1 / 2 while requiring depth before Tier 3.

## Stakeholder Drivers Analysis

### SD-1: Ministerial Council for AI and Development — Federal Delivery of the Directive

**Stakeholder**: Ministerial Council for AI and Development, chaired by HH Sheikh Mansour bin Zayed Al Nahyan.

**Driver Category**: STRATEGIC + POLITICAL

**Driver Statement**: Deliver visible, defensible federal progress toward the 50%-autonomous-services target by 2028, while preventing any single agent incident from undermining public confidence in the UAE's AI leadership.

**Context & Background**: The directive of 23 April 2026 and the Cabinet's federal framework of 18 May 2026 make the Council accountable for federal-wide progress. The Council reports into the highest levels of UAE government and into international visibility (UAE AI 2031 narrative). A high-profile failure — a biased citizen-facing decision, a PDPL breach, an unaccountable autonomous action — would damage both the directive and the wider UAE AI brand.

**Driver Intensity**: CRITICAL

**Enablers**: clear quarterly reporting on the three KPIs; published ATRS records as evidence of transparency; demonstrable contestation routes; visible workforce-redesign stories.

**Blockers**: opaque per-entity reporting; a major incident with no clear accountability; foreign-jurisdiction dependency on a Confidential dataset.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-2 (Cabinet Affairs); SD-3 (Entity Highest Authority); SD-10 (Cybersecurity Council); SD-11 (Data Office); SD-9 (citizens).

---

### SD-2: Minister of Cabinet Affairs — Visible Execution Pace

**Stakeholder**: Minister of Cabinet Affairs, Mohammad Al Gergawi — federal execution coordinator.

**Driver Category**: OPERATIONAL + POLITICAL

**Driver Statement**: Demonstrate month-over-month progress across federal entities so that the 24-month target stays credible; surface laggards early so course correction is possible.

**Context & Background**: A 24-month, 50% target is enormously ambitious. The execution coordinator needs comparable, timely data from every federal entity to keep the directive on track and to escalate where momentum is at risk.

**Driver Intensity**: CRITICAL

**Enablers**: standardised KPI reporting; cross-entity dashboards; entities that publish before being asked.

**Blockers**: entities that report only at quarter-end; metric ambiguity (what counts as "autonomous"?); over-cautious assurance functions that delay tier promotion past the point of being justifiable.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-1; SD-3; SD-4 (CAIO); SD-7 (Ministry SROs).

---

### SD-3: Entity Highest Authority — Defensible Entity Outcomes

**Stakeholder**: The accountable Minister / Director-General of the entity adopting this playbook.

**Driver Category**: ACCOUNTABILITY + REPUTATIONAL

**Driver Statement**: Stand behind every autonomous decision made by the entity's agents, in public and in front of the Ministerial Council, without surprises.

**Context & Background**: The Highest Authority signs off on the entity's contribution to the directive. Any incident — bias finding, PDPL breach, unfair work-permit decision, audit failure — lands here.

**Driver Intensity**: CRITICAL

**Enablers**: a working AI Governance Committee with the authority to halt or roll back; documented oversight evidence per agent (PRIN §I.1); the three KPIs measured and reported quarterly.

**Blockers**: unclear accountability per agent (who is the named officer?); inability to explain a given decision when challenged; the surprise of finding a Tier-3 agent operating without a published ATRS.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-1; SD-4 (CAIO is the operational counterpart); SD-7 (SROs); SD-9 (citizens, via contestation volume).

---

### SD-4: Chief AI Officer (CAIO) — Run a Sustainable, Defensible Portfolio

**Stakeholder**: The entity CAIO. This document's owner.

**Driver Category**: OPERATIONAL + PERSONAL

**Driver Statement**: Deliver the three directive KPIs at a pace that the assurance functions can support, without accumulating an unmanageable compliance debt, and without becoming the named individual on an incident report.

**Context & Background**: The CAIO is squeezed between Cabinet-Affairs pace and DPO/CISO depth. The role is new; the directive is unforgiving on the 24-month timeline. The CAIO has both career upside (success defines a new federal capability) and career downside (failure attaches to the role personally).

**Driver Intensity**: CRITICAL

**Enablers**: autonomy-tier gates that allow proportionate governance; the `/arckit:build` parallel-build accelerator for compliance artefacts; a working delegation model so not every decision lands on the CAIO's desk.

**Blockers**: Cabinet-Affairs pressure to declare a Tier-3 win prematurely; DPO/CISO requirements that arrive late in the cycle; vendor opacity that makes ATRS impossible to publish honestly.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-2 (pace); SD-5 (CISO depth); SD-6 (DPO depth); SD-7 (SRO partnership); SD-12 (vendors).

---

### SD-5: CISO — IAS Compliance and No Breach

**Stakeholder**: Entity CISO.

**Driver Category**: COMPLIANCE + RISK

**Driver Statement**: Maintain UAE IAS posture across the agentic-AI estate; prevent a breach, a prompt-injection incident, a model-extraction event, or a sovereign-cloud residency violation.

**Context & Background**: The UAE IAS is the statutory baseline (188 controls, M1–M6 + T1–T9, P1–P4). Agentic systems expand the attack surface in ways CISOs have limited precedent for. The Cybersecurity Council will eventually audit, and the entity needs to be ready.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH

**Enablers**: per-agent threat models; AI-specific test corpora; IAS SoA produced via `/arckit-uae:uae-ias`; classification-aware CI/CD gates.

**Blockers**: agents that go live without an IAS-priority-P1 control set; vendor stacks that don't permit the CISO to inspect what they need; informal "just-this-once" exceptions.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-3 (incident lands here); SD-4; SD-10 (Cybersecurity Council).

---

### SD-6: DPO / Data Office Liaison — PDPL Compliance

**Stakeholder**: Entity DPO and Data Office liaison.

**Driver Category**: COMPLIANCE + RISK

**Driver Statement**: Ensure every agent operates with a documented PDPL lawful basis, a current DPIA where required, and a working data-subject-rights process — and that no cross-border transfer happens outside an approved mechanism.

**Context & Background**: PDPL Federal Decree-Law 45/2021 is enforced by the UAE Data Office. Agents that process personal data (AI-002 tax, AI-003 citizen-facing, AI-005 work-permits) create high-stakes processing activities.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH

**Enablers**: per-agent DPIAs run early; lawful-basis register maintained; cross-border-transfer log automated; data-subject-rights process tested end-to-end.

**Blockers**: agents that go live before a DPIA; vendor foundation models whose training data may include UAE personal data without documented basis; cross-border default in supplier defaults.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-3; SD-4; SD-11 (Data Office); SD-12 (vendor opacity).

---

### SD-7: Ministry Service SROs — Service Quality and SRO Reputation

**Stakeholder**: The five SROs whose services are being automated — Federal Procurement, Federal Tax Authority audit function, citizen-service ownership (AI-003), internal-IT ownership (AI-004), MoHRE work-permit ownership.

**Driver Category**: OPERATIONAL + REPUTATIONAL

**Driver Statement**: Deliver the service better with the agent than without it — measured against the three directive KPIs and against the SRO's pre-existing service KPIs — and avoid being the SRO of record on a Charter or PDPL incident.

**Context & Background**: SROs were running these services before the directive arrived. The agent is *their* tool to deliver the directive KPI for their service. They want quality and accountability, not just speed.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH (per SRO)

**Enablers**: SRO has co-ownership of the agent's design, not just its operation; baseline service metrics protected through the transition; the operator population is genuinely trained, not just briefed.

**Blockers**: an agent declared Tier-3 without the SRO's signed acceptance; metric regression masked by enthusiasm for the new pilot; pace pressure that pushes the SRO to accept tier promotion before they are comfortable.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-3; SD-4; SD-8 (operators); SD-9 (citizens).

---

### SD-8: Federal Workforce and Operators — Future of Their Work

**Stakeholder**: The federal employees whose work the agents partly automate — procurement officers, tax auditors, service-desk staff, MoHRE case officers — and the broader 80,000-employee workforce cohort in agentic-AI training.

**Driver Category**: PERSONAL + OPERATIONAL

**Driver Statement**: Understand what their role becomes, retain or grow in capability, and not be the person taking the fall for an agent decision they couldn't realistically have overridden.

**Context & Background**: The directive's third KPI — workflow redesign — anticipates new roles, not eliminated roles. But the lived experience for an officer reviewing an agent's autonomous decision depends entirely on whether their oversight is real (PRIN §I.1) or theatre.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH

**Enablers**: real reviewer training and competence assessment; reviewer tools that surface confidence and rationale they can interrogate; explicit role description for the post-agent operator role; visible career path.

**Blockers**: tools that ask reviewers to rubber-stamp; metric pressure that punishes overrides; ambiguity about who is accountable when the agent gets it wrong.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-4; SD-7 (SRO is workforce's nearest decision-maker); FAHR (workforce policy).

---

### SD-9: Citizens, Residents, Businesses — Fair, Fast, Contestable Service

**Stakeholder**: Affected public — citizens, residents, visitors, businesses, work-permit applicants and employers, taxpayers, suppliers.

**Driver Category**: CUSTOMER + RIGHTS

**Driver Statement**: Get a fast, accurate answer; understand why if the answer is "no"; have a working route to contest a decision.

**Context & Background**: For AI-003 the relationship is light-touch (conversational service); for AI-002, AI-005, AI-001 the relationship is consequential (tax decisions, work permits, supplier evaluation). The Charter (Principles 5, 6, 11) commits the federal government to transparency, oversight, and inclusion.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH (acute for AI-002, AI-005; broad for AI-003)

**Enablers**: published ATRS in plain language, Arabic and English; per-decision rationale accessible on request; contestation route published and reachable.

**Blockers**: a "computer said no" experience with no human path; language gaps (poor Arabic-dialect coverage; secondary-language gaps); contestation queues that defeat the right.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-3 (incident lands here); SD-7 (SRO owns service quality); SD-11 (Data Office for data-subject-rights complaints).

---

### SD-10: UAE Cybersecurity Council — Federal IAS Posture

**Stakeholder**: UAE Cybersecurity Council.

**Driver Category**: REGULATORY

**Driver Statement**: Maintain a credible IAS posture across federal entities deploying agentic AI; ensure no sovereign-residency drift; produce sector-level threat intelligence.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH

**Enablers**: entities that submit IAS Statements of Applicability promptly; entities that report incidents accurately; coordinated AI-specific threat-model template at federal level.

**Blockers**: entities that minimise reporting; cross-border-residency surprises; vendor lock-in patterns that limit the Council's effective oversight.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-1; SD-5.

---

### SD-11: UAE Data Office — PDPL Enforcement and National Data Posture

**Stakeholder**: UAE Data Office.

**Driver Category**: REGULATORY

**Driver Statement**: Enforce PDPL across federal entities; build the national data-protection record; ensure cross-entity data sharing operates inside the legal framework.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH

**Enablers**: per-agent DPIAs submitted before go-live; cross-border-transfer log maintained; data-subject-rights processes operating inside statutory timelines.

**Blockers**: late DPIAs; opaque vendor data-handling; cross-entity sharing that bypasses the Data Sharing Policy.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-1; SD-6.

---

### SD-12: Foundation-Model Vendors — Commercial Relationship and IP Boundaries

**Stakeholder**: The (likely several) vendors supplying foundation models, agentic-AI platforms, retrieval components, or hosting.

**Driver Category**: COMMERCIAL

**Driver Statement**: Maintain commercial relationship; protect IP and training data; meet contractual SLAs; minimise disclosure to inspecting third parties.

**Driver Intensity**: HIGH (for them); MEDIUM (for the entity's leverage over them)

**Enablers** (for the entity): contractual change-notification rights for base-model updates; data-residency and processor commitments under PDPL; right to audit / right to bias-test; willingness to disclose training-data provenance to the extent required by the Charter's bias-mitigation principle.

**Blockers**: vendor opacity on training data (a known structural issue); base-model swaps without notice; processor clauses that don't meet PDPL Article 28 equivalents.

**Related Stakeholders**: SD-4 (CAIO depends on them); SD-5 (CISO inspects them); SD-6 (DPO contracts them).

## Driver-to-Goal Mapping

### Goal G-1: Five ATRS records published before next quarterly Ministerial Council reporting

**Derived From Drivers**: SD-1, SD-3, SD-7, SD-9.

**Goal Owner**: CAIO.

**Goal Statement**: One published ATRS-equivalent record for each of AI-001 … AI-005 (Procurement, Tax Auditing, Customer Happiness, Technical Support, MoHRE Work-Permits), in plain Arabic and English, citizen-readable, before the next quarterly reporting submission (target: 2026-08-28).

**Why This Matters**: Charter Principle 5 (Transparency) is non-defensible without ATRS for the five named live pilots. Citizen confidence (SD-9) depends on it. Ministerial Council reporting (SD-1) depends on it.

**Success Metrics**:

- **Primary**: 5 of 5 agents have a published ATRS record.
- **Secondary**: Citizen-readability test passed by a non-engineering reviewer in Arabic and English.

**Baseline**: 0 of 5.

**Target**: 5 of 5 by 2026-08-28.

**Measurement Method**: Inventory check against the published register; readability sign-off by a non-engineer.

**Dependencies**: vendor disclosure for ATRS dataset-provenance fields; SRO sign-off per agent.

**Risks to Achievement**: vendor opacity blocks honest disclosure; SRO disagreement on tier classification delays ATRS sign-off.

**Baseline**: training not yet started for new reviewer cohorts.

**Target**: 80 % of reviewer population trained and competence-assessed by 2026-08-28.

**Risks**: training quality is symbolic rather than rigorous; reviewer-role description is ambiguous.

## Goal-to-Outcome Mapping

### Outcome O-1: Adoption-speed KPI achievement (Directive KPI 1)

### Conflict Analysis

**Competing Drivers**:

- **Conflict 1 — Pace vs Depth**: SD-2 (Cabinet Affairs pace) vs SD-5 (CISO) + SD-6 (DPO) + SD-7 (SRO) depth. Cabinet Affairs is measuring monthly progress; CISO/DPO/SRO need evidence before sign-off.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: enforce the autonomy-tier gate (PRIN §I.2; Goal G-2). Pace is rewarded at Tier 1 / Tier 2; depth is required for Tier 3 promotion. This makes the trade-off explicit rather than political.
- **Conflict 2 — Citizen-readable transparency vs vendor IP**: SD-9 (citizens want plain-language ATRS) + SD-11 (Data Office wants provenance) vs SD-12 (vendor IP / training-data opacity).
  - **Resolution Strategy**: contractually require disclosure to the extent the Charter and PDPL demand; where the vendor refuses, the agent does not get Tier-3 status. Use procurement leverage via `/arckit-uae:uae-procurement`.
- **Conflict 3 — Tier-3 ambition vs Operator override authority**: SD-2 (pace toward Tier-3) vs SD-8 (operators want meaningful oversight, not rubber-stamp). The operator population has a personal stake in real overrideability.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: measure override-rate as a behavioural metric; metric pressure that punishes overrides is a leading indicator of failure (per PRIN §I.1). Quarterly review by AI Governance Committee.
- **Conflict 4 — Workforce future**: SD-8 needs reassurance that workflow-redesign means new roles, not eliminated roles; SD-2 may be tempted to claim productivity wins as net-headcount reduction.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: explicitly frame KPI 3 (workflow redesign) as capability uplift, with the 80,000-in-training cohort as the stated trajectory.

**Synergies**:

- **Synergy 1**: SD-1 (Ministerial Council) and SD-9 (citizens) both want defensible, transparent, contestable services. Investments in ATRS, contestation routes, and Arabic-language parity serve both.
- **Synergy 2**: SD-5 (CISO IAS) and SD-6 (DPO PDPL) overlap heavily — the controls implementing PDPL Article-equivalents serve IAS T-series controls. Coordinated `/arckit-uae:uae-ias` and `/arckit-uae:uae-pdpl` execution serves both.
- **Synergy 3**: SD-7 (SRO service quality) and SD-8 (operator future-of-work) both depend on the agent being good enough that real overrides happen rarely and meaningfully. Investing in reviewer competence and rationale-surfacing serves both.
